# Project Summary — Deep-Space Portfolio

A detailed walkthrough of what this portfolio is, how it's built, and the design
decisions behind it. The whole thing is a single self-contained `index_space.html`
with no external assets, no libraries, and no build step — every visual is generated
in plain JavaScript on an HTML5 canvas.

---

## 1. Concept

The brief evolved over several iterations into a clear thesis: a portfolio for a
**technical person who loves art**, aimed at **general SWE / full-stack** roles.

That framing drove two principles:

1. **The beauty should be generated by code, not sourced as a picture.** Every
   visual is hand-coded, so there's never a question of whether the art is the
   author's own — it's a running program. This is the cleanest way to express
   "engineer with taste."
2. **The art is the front door, not the whole house.** The animated scene earns a
   recruiter's attention in the first few seconds; the real substance (experience,
   projects, skills) sits one tap behind it.

The visual direction landed on **"outer space, but calm"** — a deep-space scene
that's atmospheric and alive without being loud or sci-fi kitsch.

## 3. The generative space scene

Everything below is drawn each frame on a single full-screen canvas, layered back
to front so depth reads correctly.

### Starfield (two layers)

- A **background drift layer** of small faint stars that slowly pan and twinkle,
  giving the sky depth.
- A **constellation layer** of larger stars that connect with thin lines when
  near each other. These stars are **idle** (fixed in place like a real sky) and
  each takes a **natural stellar color** drawn from a temperature palette —
  warm ambers and oranges for cool stars through white to blue-white for hot ones.
  Brighter stars get a soft glow and a cross-sparkle.
- **The world scales with the viewport.** Star positions are stored in **normalized
  (0–1) coordinates** and converted to pixels each frame, so the whole scene — stars,
  sun, planets, astronaut — stretches and compresses together as the window resizes.
  Stars are seeded **once**; resizing just remaps them rather than re-randomizing the
  sky, so expanding or shrinking the window no longer reshuffles the stars.

### The sun

- A warm glowing orb in the upper-right, built from two radial gradients (a bright
  core and a wide halo that fades from gold through faint violet into the night).
- Pulses very slowly (a ~4% "breathe") so it feels alive rather than static.
- **Eclipse falloff:** stars fade out smoothly as they approach the sun and
  reappear in the darker sky beyond it, so the sun commands its own region of sky.

### Orbiting planets

- Two planets travel on **elliptical orbits** around the sun (a small blue-grey
  world and a larger amber ringed planet), each with its own radius, speed, size,
  and color.
- Faint orbit paths are traced around the sun.
- **Depth handling:** a planet on the far side of its orbit draws *behind* the
  sun's glow; on the near side it draws *in front* — so it reads as genuine 3D
  orbiting rather than flat motion.

### The astronaut

- A **penguin in an astronaut suit**, hand-drawn from canvas shapes with a true
  penguin silhouette: an oval white suit body, small wing-like flippers hugging the
  sides, little orange webbed feet, a chest control panel, a life-support backpack,
  a soft glow ring, and a domed helmet whose visor frames the penguin's face —
  white mask, eyes, and orange beak.
- Has **true free-floating momentum**: a real position and velocity rather than a
  fixed path. It wanders gently on its own via a soft drifting force.
- **Bumpable:** moving the cursor into it transfers momentum and a little spin,
  genuinely knocking it off course — it then coasts in the new direction (very low
  friction, like zero-g) until it drifts elsewhere or softly bounces off a screen
  edge. A speed cap keeps it from being flung uncontrollably.

### Asteroids (depth-aware, screen-persistent)

- Spawn in **showers** at random intervals — a cluster streaking across at an angle.
  A population cap (`A_CAP`) keeps the field from over-filling; showers only top it up
  to the cap and refill it as asteroids leave.
- Each asteroid carries a **z (depth)** value and a depth velocity:
  - Some fly **from deep in the screen toward the viewer** (growing as they approach).
  - Others start **near and recede into deep space** (shrinking).
- On-screen size and apparent speed scale with depth; asteroids draw far-to-near so
  closer ones correctly overlap distant ones.
- **Three exit rules govern where an asteroid can leave:**
  - **Side borders → wrap.** Crossing an edge (plus an off-screen buffer) re-enters
    from the opposite edge instead of vanishing.
  - **Receding into the distance → exits.** An asteroid whose depth grows past the far
    threshold (`A_ZFAR`) shrinks away and is culled — the one place it's allowed to
    disappear.
  - **Toward the viewer → bounces.** The near plane (`A_ZMIN`) is a hard wall: an
    approaching asteroid reaches a maximum size, reverses its depth velocity, and
    sails back out rather than flying past the viewer.
- **Depth-gated collision:** an asteroid can only knock the astronaut while it's
  still *behind* the astronaut's depth plane. The collision is **re-armable** — once
  the asteroid clears the astronaut (or drops behind the plane) it can bump again on a
  later pass, instead of being a one-shot hit.
- No motion trails — clean rocky shapes only.

## 4. Typography & palette

- **Font:** Sora (Google Fonts), used as a single family throughout for a unified,
  calm, modern-space-UI feel. The hero name sits at a lighter weight with slightly
  tighter tracking so it reads airy rather than heavy.
- **Palette:** deep-void blues for the background, soft starlight whites and
  periwinkle for text, and a cool blue-violet accent. Cards are dark translucent
  "glass" via `backdrop-filter: blur()`.

---

## 5. Accessibility & performance

- **Reduced motion:** with `prefers-reduced-motion`, the drift, bump, asteroid
  showers, and other animation settle into a calm static scene; the astronaut
  parks in a fixed pose.
- **Keyboard & semantics:** menu buttons use `aria-expanded`, panels are labelled
  regions, and focus rings stay visible.
- **Responsive:** because positions are normalized, resizing **stretches or compresses
  the same world** rather than reseeding it — the scene stays coherent from phones to
  ultrawide displays, and the astronaut keeps its relative spot. The project grid and
  layout collapse cleanly on mobile.
- **Self-contained & light:** one HTML file, no libraries, no network requests
  beyond the Google Fonts stylesheet. The only raster image — the profile photo in
  *about me* — is embedded inline as an optimized base64 data URI, so there are still
  no external assets and nothing that can break from a missing file.

## 6. Tuning reference

Most of the scene's feel can be adjusted via small constants in the script:

- **Stars:** the `stellar` color array (bias warmer or cooler); star counts in the
  seed functions (positions are normalized, seeded once).
- **Sun:** `core` (size), the halo/orb gradient stops (warmth), `eclipse` radius
  (how far the star-free zone extends).
- **Planets:** the `planets` array — `rad` (orbit distance), `speed`, `r` (size),
  `col`, `ring`; the `0.42` flattening factor controls orbit tilt.
- **Astronaut:** wander-force terms, bump strength, `max` speed, friction, and edge
  bounce.
- **Asteroids:** `spawnShower` controls count, speed, and the inward/outward depth
  ranges; `nextShower` sets the interval; `A_CAP` caps the field size; `A_BUF` is the
  off-screen wrap buffer; `A_ZMIN` is the near bounce plane and `A_ZFAR` the far exit
  threshold; `ASTRO_Z` is the collision depth plane.
